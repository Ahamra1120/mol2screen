Insulin

Insulin is a vital hormone produced by the pancreas that regulates blood glucose (sugar) levels. It acts like a "key" that allows glucose from the bloodstream to enter the body's cells, providing them with energy.

The primary function of insulin is to maintain a stable blood sugar range and prevent hyperglycemia (high blood sugar). This involves several processes:

  • Glucose Uptake: When blood glucose levels rise after a meal, the pancreas releases insulin. Insulin then signals muscle, fat, and liver cells to absorb glucose from the blood.
  • Energy Use and Storage: Once inside the cells, glucose is used for immediate energy. Excess glucose is converted into glycogen and stored in the liver and muscles for future use. Insulin also promotes the synthesis of fats and proteins.
  • Inhibiting Glucose Production: Insulin helps reduce the liver's production and release of more glucose into the bloodstream.
  • Balancing Glucagon: Insulin works in opposition to glucagon, another hormone from the pancreas that raises blood sugar levels when they get too low (e.g., between meals or during fasting).
